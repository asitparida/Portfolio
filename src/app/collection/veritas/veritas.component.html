Research

What

is the problem ?

Dictionary.com’s word of year for 2018 is “misinformation". We are trying to mitigate the dissonance created by the "fake" news around us. But such malicious content is propagated through our social circles.

Why

is it worth solving ?

The rampant spread of “fake” news is due to people seeking out and believing information which aligns with their currently-held attitudes. We blindly trust the source, i.e., a friend of ours who re-tweeted or shared the article from somewhere (ELM).

How

are we solving it ?

We are designing an avatar-based mobile game to empower folks against such misinformation. As they progress through this embedded game design, they learn to look at content critically. We are employing multiple persuasive design strategies like fictionalization and obfuscation. We are designing an avatar based mobile game to empower folks against such misinformation.

Literature Review

We went through research papers we spoke about the different pathways people receive misinformation and essential characteristics of misinformation, especially in the form of blurbs that are “convenient,” “efficient,” and “trustworthy” sources. We took into account papers which spoke of how virtual pets influenced people and how they serve as catalysts for behavior change.

Tenet #1

People are susceptible to misinformation due to confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and the sleeper effect.

Tenet #2

Employ fictionalization and intermixing to prevent psychological reactance.

Tenet #3

Utilizing personalization techniques increase the efficacy of the persuasive message and connection with the user.

Game Design

Game Design

We created a fictional world in which users are asked to take care of a creature, affectionately called a Worble. Throughout the game, users receive information from friends in their pet community on how to take care of their Worble. The correct information will help their Worble grow whereas the malicious ones will make it sick. Users accelerate their experience points as they correctly differentiate between the actual and false information.

Paper Prototyping

The brainstorming sessions put us on a path towards the Tamagotchi-like game concept. We created multiple fidelities for our initial designs. To help us validate the needs we were addressing, we conducted think-aloud sessions on our paper prototypes.

Digital Prototyping

Choosing React-Native

With the scope of the prototype decided and the game sceens fleshed out, the next task was to pick up the right framework to build the initial version for the app and test with participants. Building on my experience of working in cross-functional teams, I started gathering all the data points to help the team choose the most affordable toolkit.

Web vs Native

We had to go native because however well the web version of the game could be built, the look and feel of an native app, executed correctly, would accentuate the game experience.

Learning curve for team

The entire team wanted to contribute in coding. I felt react-native offered a little bit of familiarity as their most recent experience with prototyping had come in the form of the HTML5 programming class.

HTML5 Syntax

Using the similar mark-up syntax offered the structural scaffolding for us and not learn the entire visual hierarchy of the app development process.

App Lifecycle

I broke down the overall structure into three parts so as to ease interactions flows. The standalone pages would be active one at a time with other elements either overlaying full-page or partially. Throughout the game, user had to be aware of the current context. This translated to managing the state of the game as close as possible. Mirroring the app lifecyle to game design was essential to me.

Game States to UI States

With this my first game design, I learnt how most of the game development involved reflecting the current state in the game pipeline.

Flex based layouts

Perhaps the sweetest thing about reusing the know-how of HTML5 structures, thinking of every UI frame and component in terms of flex-based layout helped in building responsivenes out-of-box.

Using Observables for pipeline

Since the app was a prototype with no back-end, I decided to go for a emitter-subscriber pattern fits in well as the same can be updated to listen to back-end events and update the game UI. I used RxJS based reactive programming for triggering several microinteractions.

During state activation

When any state gets activated, the game UI is updated, the available actions get updated and the subsequent event listeners are setup.

Triggering Event

This is achieved through taking an action, for e.g. feeding the worble, shaking the phone. Both UI clicks and native sensor actions can trigger events.

Applying Motion

Once the flow of the game was in place, I started animating components to direct user's attention towards it. The same interactions provide the necessary fluidity to the UI in general.

iOS App

Further, I used native iOS capabilities, like using the accelerometer for detecting a shake, to enhance the game experience. After multiple iterations of design and parallel development cycles, we tested our app. We had favourable reviews with excellent critiques from the studio audience.
